# Epistemic Awareness
- Understanding the reliability and trustworthiness of LLMs is increasingly important as they are used in areas such as:
  - Scientific discovery
  - Medical diagnosis
  - Legal consulting

- Despite their strong capabilities, LLMs still experience **hallucinations**.

- Hallucinations involve LLMs generating false claims, often presented in confident and assertive language.

- This mismatch between confidence and accuracy can create risks in real-world applications.

- Over-confident or under-confident responses can:
  - Mislead users
  - Reduce trust
  - Encourage over-reliance on LLM-based systems

- Measuring model uncertainty has become an important way to:
  - Support AI-assisted decision-making
  - Reduce potential harm
  - Improve the safe use of LLMs

## 1. Can LLMs Use Linguistic Uncertainty Markers to Reliably Reflect Intrinsic Confidence?
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.28778

### Introduction
- Traditional uncertainty measurements rely on numerical representations. These methods are limited in that they lLack of alignment with natural communication and computational expense (especially if dependent on multiple model calls or auxiliary networks).
- *Hedging phrases* or *Epistemic Markers* to signal uncertainty has therefore gained more attention.
    - Human aligned
    - Improves human judgement
    -  linguistic confidence expression is known to improve user judgments of LLM credibility, boost task accuracy in human-AI teaming, and incur minimal computational overhead.
    - Recent work focused on enabling LLMs to use natural language to faithfully express their intinsic uncertainty, which may be estimated via *sampling consistency*.
- It is unclear whether LLMs consistently use **epistemic markers** to express specific confidence levels.
- Prior work shows LLMs often fail to express their internal confidence accurately in words.
- However, these studies usually assume LLMs should use uncertainty language the same way humans do.
- Human interpretations of hedging language also vary across people and contexts.
- A model may use words like “perhaps” differently from humans, but still use them consistently.
- This means epistemic markers could still reflect a model’s internal confidence, even if they do not match human intuition.
- The study tests this **model-internal calibration**, which prior work has not examined.

## Gap
- Whether LLMs **epistemic markers** reliably reflect their own internal confidence
    - Prior work focused on how uncertainty markers relate to external accuracy.
- Defines *marker internal confidence* ('MIC')
- It also considers how this relationship changes across:
  - Task domain
  - Task format
  - Task difficulty
- The study finds that LLMs do **not** consistently apply their own linguistic confidence framework.
